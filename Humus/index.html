Humus

A regenerative ecosystem for community prosperity

Seeds, Plants & Mycelium (SPaM) — autonomous nodes, network canopy, and tax-aware legal flows

Vision

A world where economic security is a shared human right, supported through interconnected, regenerative communities. Stability emerges from shared prosperity and systemic support, not scarcity or centralization.

Mission

To create a cooperative ecosystem in which members pool resources, access predictable support, and enable network growth. Humus allows resources to flow transparently between communities (Plants), supporting both current members and the emergence of new nodes.

Overview

Abstract

Humus is a network of autonomous communities (Plants) that pool resources, share labor, and propagate resilience through an interconnected network (Mycelium). Members (Seeds) contribute according to their means, receive equitable support, and may seed new nodes. The ecosystem leverages SPaM (Seeds, Plants, Mycelium) as an internal metaphor while operationalizing contributions, grants, and resource flows across 501(d) and 501(c)(3) structures, creating a tax-aware, legally compliant, and scalable network.

SPaM Metaphor

The Canopy is the Mycelium: the network connecting nodes, redistributing resources, supporting propagation, and weaving the whole ecosystem together.

  • Seeds Individuals or small groups initiating participation. Their labor, contributions, and ideas form the basis of community growth.
  • Plants Local communities (typically structured as 501(d)s, LLCs, or co-ops) that pool resources, provide mutual aid, and develop shared infrastructure.
  • Mycelium (Canopy) The network connecting Plants, redistributing resources, providing guidance, supporting replication, and enabling systemic resilience.

Key principle: Some Seeds will flourish into Plants, others may fail, and resources from dormant or exited nodes recycle into the Mycelium to support new growth.

Legal and Tax Architecture

Node-level: 501(d) Plants

  • Members pool income, labor, or investment-equivalent wealth.
  • Members may work for the 501(d), meaning earned contributions through the node are not taxed individually.
  • Plants can distribute funds for members’ basic needs (housing, food, utilities, education) under IRS rules.
  • Nodes can support exiting members to seed new Plants, maintaining network propagation.

Network-level: Mycelium (501(c)(3) or coordinating body)

  • Can accept tax-deductible donations, including cash, stock, or other assets.
  • Redistributes resources to Plants or directly to members through grants or program-related support.
  • Provides system-level coordination (e.g., replication funding, shared resources, technical guidance) while preserving autonomy of each Plant.
  • Serves as the network tissue, ensuring resource flows between nodes and supporting growth and stability.

Hybrid or nonprofit-adjacent nodes

The network can include LLCs, co-ops, or informal collectives. Legal flexibility allows nodes to participate and receive support without all being formal 501(d)s. Tax and legal compliance are maintained by structuring transfers, grants, and resource allocations appropriately.

Resource Flows

  • Seed contribution Individual Seeds contribute labor, wealth, or other resources to their Plant.
  • Plant allocation Resources are pooled and distributed according to local needs; administrative overhead is minimized.
  • Plant-to-Mycelium Plants contribute to the Mycelium to support other nodes, replication, and systemic infrastructure.
  • Mycelium-to-network The Mycelium redistributes pooled resources across Plants and, when needed, directly to Seeds, ensuring network-wide stability.
  • Propagation support Exiting Seeds or dormant nodes may receive funding or guidance to start new Plants, turning exits into network growth.

Core principle: The system balances autonomy, reciprocity, and propagation, allowing resource flows to follow ecological rules rather than strict proportionality.

Governance and Participation

Plant governance

Each community sets internal rules, labor contribution expectations, and dispute resolution mechanisms.

Mycelium governance

Provides network-level guidance, resource allocation formulas, and replication support; operates transparently and democratically.

Transparency

Resource flows, rules, and eligibility criteria are visible to all members.

Propagation incentives

The system encourages seeding new Plants as the measure of growth rather than retention of members or resources.

Philosophical Principles

  • Regeneration Resources and knowledge are recycled to support emerging nodes.
  • Propagation Success is measured by network expansion and member flourishing, not individual accumulation.
  • Human-centered ecology The network is modeled after natural ecosystems — adaptive, resilient, and interdependent.
  • Transparency and trust Members understand system mechanics even if exact flows vary dynamically.

Financial and Tax Considerations

  • Members Earned income contributed to Plants via work for the 501(d) is not taxed as individual income.
  • 501(d) Plants Can pool, invest, and disburse funds; distributions for basic needs follow IRS rules to minimize taxes.
  • Mycelium (501(c)(3)) Accepts deductible donations, including stocks or assets, and redistributes resources to Plants or Seeds according to network rules.
  • Investments Pooled funds can be invested at node or network level; distributions to members are taxable upon receipt, but growth accrues within the node until disbursed.

This overview is descriptive, not individualized tax or legal advice.

Branding and Culture

Brand: Humus — fertile ground for human flourishing, growth, and shared prosperity.

Internal metaphor: SPaM — Seeds, Plants, Mycelium — propagation, interconnection, and systemic support.

Messaging: Regeneration, networked support, and community resilience.

Tagline examples

  • “Where communities take root and thrive.”
  • “Fertile ground for mutual aid.”
  • “The human ecosystem, growing together.”

Key Advantages

  • Multi-node, self-propagating ecosystem.
  • Transparent, predictable, yet flexible resource flows.
  • Legal and tax-aware design through 501(d), 501(c)(3), LLCs, and co-ops.
  • Focused on propagation and replication rather than retention.
  • The SPaM metaphor grounds the system in resilience, growth, and shared prosperity.

Conclusion

Humus is more than a mutual aid network — it is a living ecosystem where Seeds grow into Plants, and the Mycelium connects communities for resilience and systemic prosperity. By combining autonomous nodes, network coordination, and SPaM-inspired flows, Humus transforms mutual aid from static resource sharing into self-propagating, regenerative support for human flourishing.
